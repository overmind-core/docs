A policy document is a human-readable specification that tells the Overmind Optimizer what your agent is supposed to do — and what it must never do. Policies are not just a scoring rubric: they encode the business rules the optimizer cannot violate. Every stage of the pipeline — evaluation criteria, test data synthesis, diagnosis prompts, code generation constraints, and LLM-as-Judge scoring — is guided by your policy. An improvement that raises the average score but breaks a business rule is automatically rejected.

## What policies control

Your policy document feeds directly into the following stages of the optimization pipeline:

- **Evaluation criteria** — The eval spec is derived from your policy, so scoring reflects your actual requirements rather than generic correctness.
- **Test data synthesis** — When Overmind generates synthetic test cases, it draws scenarios from your policy's edge cases and decision rules.
- **Diagnosis prompts** — The analyzer model receives your policy when identifying failure patterns, so it understands the intent behind expected behavior.
- **Code generation constraints** — Candidate fixes are generated with your policy's constraints in scope, preventing the optimizer from introducing changes that satisfy test cases but violate domain rules.
- **LLM-as-Judge scoring rubrics** — When semantic scoring is enabled, the judge model uses your policy's quality expectations to evaluate outputs it cannot check mechanically.

## Policy document structure

A policy document is a Markdown file with a set of named sections. Here is a complete example for a lead qualification agent:

```markdown
# Agent Policy: Lead Qualification

## Purpose
Qualifies inbound sales leads by analyzing company data and inquiry content.

## Decision Rules
1. If the inquiry mentions "enterprise" or "custom pricing", classify as hot
2. Companies with 500+ employees get a minimum lead score of 60
3. Inquiries about specific product features indicate warm interest

## Constraints
- Never disqualify without checking company size
- Score and category must be consistent (hot = 70+, warm = 40-69, cold = <40)
- Reasoning must reference specific data points from the input

## Priority Order
1. Accuracy of category classification
2. Score calibration
3. Reasoning quality

## Edge Cases
| Scenario             | Expected Behaviour                     |
|----------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Missing company name | Default to cold, note in reasoning     |
| Competitor inquiry   | Classify as cold, recommend nurture    |

## Quality Expectations
- Reasoning should reference specific data points from the input
- Scores should be calibrated: hot leads 70-100, warm 40-69, cold 0-39
```

The sections you include are flexible, but **Decision Rules**, **Constraints**, **Priority Order**, and **Edge Cases** have the most direct impact on optimization behavior. The more specific these sections are, the more precisely the optimizer can target genuine improvements.

## Providing or generating a policy

When you run `/overmind-generate-spec-and-dataset`, the skill asks whether you have an existing policy document:

- **If you have a policy**, provide the path to your Markdown file. Overmind analyzes it against your agent code and suggests refinements to align the policy with what the code actually does.
- **If you don't have a policy**, Overmind infers one automatically from your agent code and agent description.

In both cases, you review the policy in a conversational refinement loop. The assistant presents the current draft, explains its choices, and accepts your edits before finalizing. Nothing is saved until you approve.

Once finalized, the policy is pushed to the Overmind backend. You can view and edit it at any time from [console.overmindlab.ai/agents](https://console.overmindlab.ai/agents).

<Note>
  **Policies and eval specs are related but distinct.** Your policy document is human-readable and expresses intent in plain language. The `/overmind-generate-spec-and-dataset` skill derives a machine-readable eval spec from it — with precise field names, scoring weights, tolerance bands, and tool-call checks. You can edit both the policy and the eval spec from the console. Changes take effect on the next optimization run.
</Note>

<Tip>
  **Tips for writing effective policies:**

  - **Be specific about decision rules.** Vague rules like "classify correctly" give the optimizer little to work with. Concrete rules like "companies with 500+ employees get a minimum lead score of 60" produce much better results.
  - **Include your edge cases.** The optimizer synthesizes test data from your policy's edge cases. If an edge case isn't in the policy, it won't be tested.
  - **Specify priority order.** When multiple criteria are in tension, the optimizer needs to know which one to favor. List them explicitly under a **Priority Order** section.
  - **Use numeric thresholds.** Wherever your domain has quantitative boundaries — score ranges, confidence levels, size cutoffs — write them down explicitly. "Hot leads score 70–100" is far more actionable than "hot leads score high."
</Tip>
